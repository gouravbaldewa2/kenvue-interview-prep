1Tell me about yourself and your career journey+

Use the scripted 2-minute opener (see Opener Script section). Key beats:

  • Open with identity: “Brand and innovation leader, 11 years across large FMCG and startups”
  • Colgate foundation: Trade fundamentals + Vedshakti launch (8.9 share, health-claim product)

  • Close with why: “Intersection of hydration knowledge, test-and-learn discipline, startup scrappiness, and big-company scale — plus purpose: products that improve health outcomes.”

Keep it under 2 minutes. Don’t recite your resume — tell a story with a through-line.

2Why Kenvue? Why this role?+
  • Pattern in career: “I’ve always been drawn to building new things. Left corporate for Chai Point and Merhaki. At Coca-Cola, my proudest work was the new: Sprite Zero, At-Home occasion, rural model, Rapid Hydration blueprint.”
  • Natural next step: “Full-time innovation leadership, in hydration — a category I know — with a build-from-ground-up mandate.”
  • Purpose: “Products that improve health outcomes for millions of Indian families. Diarrhea is the 3rd leading cause of death in children under 5 — ORS coverage is only 60.6%. That’s a problem worth solving.”
  • Dual-brand excitement: “ORSL and eRZL represent a textbook portfolio strategy — medical credibility and lifestyle appeal. I want to accelerate both.”

Never badmouth Coca-Cola. Frame as “running toward something,” not away.

3What’s the biggest risk we’d be taking by hiring you?+

Name the real gap — HCP engagement — then immediately shrink it:

  • “I haven’t done medical detailing or direct HCP engagement. That’s the gap, and I take it seriously.”
  • Shrink it: “But I’m not starting from zero. I launched Colgate Vedshakti — an Ayurvedic health-claim product. I built food & nutrition categories at Merhaki. And at Coca-Cola, I conceptualised a Rapid Hydration product launch blueprint.”
  • Show a plan: “My first 30 days: embed with medical affairs, sit in on HCP interactions, study how Electral and Cipla engage doctors. I’ve learned new stakeholder languages before — bottlers at Coca-Cola, retailers at Colgate.”
  • Reframe the portfolio: “eRZL is consumer/retail-led — that’s squarely my strength. ORSL is HCP-led — I’d lean on existing medical affairs capability while building my own fluency.”

This is the elephant-in-the-room question. Owning the gap with a plan shows maturity. Dodging it is the real risk.

Round 2 — Innovation & Strategy 20 min
4How would you build an innovation pipeline for the hydration portfolio?+

Structure around 3 horizons:

  • Start with insight: Consumer ethnography, social listening, HCP interviews. Map unmet needs across occasions — illness, sports, everyday wellness, travel, children, elderly.
  • H1 (0-12 months): Line extensions on ORSL/eRZL — new flavours, new pack sizes, seasonal variants. Quick wins that build momentum.
  • H2 (12-24 months): Adjacent innovations — children’s hydration, hydration + immunity, sports-specific formulation. Bigger bets with higher potential.
  • H3 (24-36 months): Breakthrough — personalised hydration, dissolvable strips, tech-enabled tracking, new delivery formats like gummies.
  • Process: Stage-gate with clear go/no-go criteria (desirability, feasibility, viability). Innovation council with R&D, supply chain, regulatory, and commercial.
  • Personal proof: “This is exactly how I approached Sprite Zero — insight led to hypothesis, 6-city pilot, measured PMF, then scaled. I’d apply the same rigour here with the added science validation layer.”
5The market is getting crowded. What’s your competitive strategy?+
  • Science moat: ORSL’s WHO compliance and Hydra-Activ Technology is a defensible advantage that HUL Liquid IV, Reliance RasKik, and lifestyle brands cannot replicate. Double down on it.
  • HCP channel: Doctor recommendations are the strongest purchase trigger for health products. Invest in medical detailing — this is the one channel FMCG entrants can’t easily build.
  • Occasion ownership: Own “illness recovery” (ORSL) and “smart daily hydration” (eRZL) as distinct occasions. Don’t let the brands blur.
  • Innovation velocity: Stay ahead with new formats, formulations, and consumer experiences. If we’re the ones launching children’s hydration or hydration + immunity first, we set the standard.
  • Price-pack architecture: Rs 10 sachet to Rs 50 RTD bottle. Compete with Electral on clinical trust AND with Reliance on accessibility.
  • Rural expansion: “ORS coverage is only 60.6% for children under 5. There’s a massive untapped market in rural and Tier 2/3 India. I’ve built rural engagement models that work — 24% engagement rate at Coca-Cola, 3x benchmark.”
6Walk me through how you’d launch a new hydration product for Indian summers+

Structure as a 6-step launch playbook:

  1. Consumer insight (Month 1-2): What’s the unmet summer hydration need? Ethnographic research in high-heat markets. Is this medical (heatstroke prevention) or lifestyle (daily refreshment)? That determines ORSL vs. eRZL positioning.
  2. Formulation & regulatory (Month 2-4): Work with R&D on a summer-specific formulation — perhaps added electrolytes for heat resilience. Ensure FSSAI compliance. If making health claims, build the clinical evidence.
  3. Price-pack design (Month 3-4): Single-serve RTD for impulse (convenience stores, chemists). Multi-pack for home stocking. Consider a powder sachet at Rs 10-15 for mass accessibility.
  4. Pilot (Month 5-6): Launch in 3 high-heat cities (e.g., Nagpur, Ahmedabad, Chennai). Define success metrics: trial rate, repeat rate, velocity, HCP recommendation rate. 90-day read.
  5. HCP activation: Sampling with paediatricians and GPs in pilot cities. Position as “recommended by doctors for summer dehydration.” This is the credibility layer that lifestyle brands lack.
  6. Scale decision (Month 8): If pilot metrics hit thresholds, national rollout for the following summer. Pre-season trade loading, media plan, digital campaigns targeting mothers and fitness enthusiasts.

Bridge: “This is the exact playbook I used for Sprite Zero — pilot in select cities, measure PMF, then scale. The difference here is adding HCP validation and clinical credibility to the process.”

Round 3 — Behavioural & Leadership 15 min
7Tell me about a time you launched something new. What worked and what didn’t?+

Best story: Sprite Zero (STAR format)

  • S: Coca-Cola India had no zero-sugar Sprite variant. Health-conscious consumers were leaving sparkling for healthier options.
  • T: Lead end-to-end product development and pilot launch to test viability before committing national resources.
  • A: Cross-functional work on formulation with R&D. Selected 6 pilot cities based on health-consciousness indices. Designed the measurement framework: trial rate, repeat purchase rate, velocity. Managed the GTM, trade activation, and sampling.
  • R: 23% repeat purchase rate — a strong PMF signal. This enabled the national rollout decision.

What worked: Rigorous pilot framework with clear kill criteria. Cross-functional alignment early.

What I’d do differently: “I’d have invested more in consumer education upfront. Zero-sugar needs a trust-building narrative, especially in India where consumers associate ‘diet’ with compromise on taste. The repeat rate was strong, but trial rate could have been higher with better messaging.”

Always include “what I’d do differently” without being asked. It shows self-awareness.

8Give me an example of influencing a senior stakeholder who initially disagreed with you+

Best story: Nepal/Bangladesh growth blueprints

  • S: Regional leadership was sceptical about allocating resources to South-West Asian markets. India was the priority, and Nepal/Bangladesh were seen as low-return.
  • T: Build a compelling, data-driven case that these markets represented outsized growth potential relative to investment.
  • A: Synthesised market research, competitive data, and consumer insights into actionable growth blueprints. Framed the opportunity in the language regional leadership cared about: share gain potential, incremental volume, and competitive vulnerability if we didn’t act. For Bangladesh specifically, identified Ramadan as a culturally unique occasion and proposed Sprite Lemon Mint as a targeted play.
  • R: Got approval and budget. +1.6 share gain in Nepal, +2.7 in Bangladesh. Sprite Lemon Mint added 50% first-time Sprite consumers in Bangladesh.

Key lesson: “Understand what the stakeholder cares about, then frame your case in their language. I didn’t just present data — I showed them the cost of inaction.”

9Tell me about a time you had to make a decision with incomplete data+

Best story: Chai Point packaged foods launch

  • S: Chai Point wanted to enter packaged foods but had zero category data, no prior launches, and no established supply chain for food products. We were a tea retail chain — packaged food was completely new territory.
  • T: Decide which sub-categories to launch, which channels to prioritise, and how to allocate a limited budget — all with no internal benchmarks.
  • A: Used proxy signals instead of perfect data: analysed D2C platforms for trending food categories, studied what consumers were already ordering with tea, talked to 50+ customers directly. Made a bet on 4 sub-categories that had the best signal-to-noise ratio. Prioritised D2C and e-commerce channels (faster feedback loops) over retail (slower, more expensive).
  • R: Launched 4 sub-categories, scaled to INR 30M revenue. Not all 4 worked equally — 2 were clear winners, 1 was marginal, 1 we eventually deprioritised. The channel bet on D2C first was right — it gave us real-time consumer signals to iterate faster.

Key lesson: “In innovation, you rarely have perfect data. The skill is knowing what signal is ‘good enough’ to make a directional bet, while building in feedback loops to course-correct fast. At Kenvue, I’d apply the same principle — pilot quickly, measure obsessively, iterate or kill based on real signal.”

Round 4 — Closing 10 min
10What questions do you have for us?+

Pick 2-3 from this list depending on the conversation flow. Don’t ask all 5.

  1. “What does success look like for this role in Year 1?”
    Why it works: Shows you’re outcome-oriented and want to align expectations early. Reveals what the hiring manager truly prioritises.
  2. “How does the India innovation agenda connect to Kenvue’s global/APAC hydration strategy?”
    Why it works: Shows you’re thinking beyond India. Signals strategic ambition and awareness that this role has APAC visibility.
  3. “What’s the current relationship between the hydration portfolio and HCPs, and how do you see that evolving?”
    Why it works: Proactively addresses your gap. Shows you’re already thinking about HCP engagement rather than avoiding it.
  4. “What’s been the biggest lesson from recent innovation efforts in this portfolio — what worked and what didn’t?”
    Why it works: Shows humility and a learning mindset. Also gives you intel on what to expect on Day 1.
  5. “How does Kenvue think about the balance between innovation speed and regulatory rigour in India?”
    Why it works: Shows you understand the consumer health nuance — you can’t just “move fast and break things” with health products.

Never say “No, I think you covered everything.” Always have questions. It signals genuine interest and preparation.
